WHAT IS "PLASTIC-WATCH"?

We want to change how people think about eating out. Do you go to restaurants and automatically get plastic straws in your drinks? Or plastic kid’s cups? Or take-out containers that come in plastic bags with extra plastic utensils that immediately get thrown in the trash? All of this “extra” plastic is a social norm for us…and most of it ends up in the ocean! Sea turtles, whales, fish, and birds are just a few of the marine creatures that end up ingesting or tangled up in this plastic.

With the PlasticWatch project, we are partnering with local restaurants in Maryland to offer alternatives to these products, such as biodegradable straws or the option of having no straw at all! We also want to educate people about how their daily choices impact their local environment. Take our survey and learn about some of these impacts, while enrolling in a chance to win a free boat ride on a research cruise on the Patuxent River!

What is plastic and what does it do to the environment?

Plastic is an organic polymer made up of long, well-packed carbon chains that makes it almost impossible to break down on its own. When rain or strong winds come through, they blow over trashcans or wash trash from the streets or highways into local waterways. These waterways lead to the Chesapeake Bay and the ocean, where animals like sea turtles and birds mistake plastic trash for food. Large pieces of plastic like bags and straws can cause obstructions in the airways and digestive systems of these animals, leading to death. Small pieces of plastic, called “microplastics”, can accumulate in their stomaches and lead to eventual starvation and sickness as they become unable to store food and absorb toxic chemicals from the plastic. Plastic also floats in the ocean, getting caught up in the currents and forming “trash islands” that transport invasive species.

Lifecycle of Plastic

One of the biggest problems with plastic is that it does not degrade well. Making more and more plastic to meet consumer demand results in an accumulation of plastic that has nowhere to go! The solution? Reduce, Reuse, and Recycle! When thinking about alternative products for plastic and their environmental impact, a life cycle analysis shows the environmental cost of making a product. The life of a piece of plastic follows a 6-stage process: getting the ingredients via a supplier, transporting the ingredients, manufacturing the ingredients into a product, packaging the product, using the product, and disposing of the product. For many plastics, the disposal portion of this lifecycle can take over 1000 years. For biodegradable plastics, this can be shortened to less than a year. If you introduce recycling, the disposal portion can be skipped and the plastics can be made to make new products without using the first three stages of the life cycle, which are costly for the environment.
